# Reviving A Lost Feature

Those of us who have been in the industry long enough would recall [Legacy Final Cut Pro 7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Final_Cut_Pro#Final_Cut_Pro_4_to_7). It was a professional video editing software developed by Apple. Final Cut Pro 7 was highly popular and widely used in the film and television industry for its advanced features and powerful tools. It allowed users to edit and manipulate video footage, audio, and graphics in real-time, making it a favourite among video editors who demanded high-speed and efficient workflows. In 2011, Final Cut Pro 7 was replaced with the highly controversial announcement of Final Cut Pro X; which is simply called **Final Cut Pro** now.

[Cinema Tools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cinema_Tools) was one necessary companion application from the Final Cut Pro 7 era. It allowed the user to manage film-related metadata and export structured film lists from a timeline, for conforming, collaborating with lab and post teams, or sharing edit information with clients. The user could build a film database around their project and generate lists tailored to the deliverables each department required.

Once the user had prepared a sequence for export, you could generate a film list using `Export Film Lists` or `Export XML Film Lists`. The export dialog let you choose list format, the video track to base the list on, and which items to include—as PDF for easy reading and printing, or as XML for exchange with other software in the post-production pipeline.

![Export Film Lists](/assets/fcp7-01.png)

When you exported a film list, Cinema Tools included cut events, optical effects, transitions, filter effects, and motion effects, as well as their position on the timeline. PDF-format lists could be customised with item selection, column order, orientation, and font size, and settings could be saved and loaded across systems. This allowed collaborators to reference specific points in the project and stay organised throughout the editing process.

In Final Cut Pro, there is the widely used feature of [Export XML](https://support.apple.com/en-sg/guide/final-cut-pro/verdbd66ae/mac). Final Cut Pro is extremely powerful for handling metadata. Export XML contains most of the timeline’s information, including roles, markers, keywords, transitions, effects, and more. However, it is not easily accessible from the end user’s perspective as a structured production spreadsheet. Hence, I would not associate Final Cut Pro’s Export XML as a direct feature parity with legacy Cinema Tools film list export.

**Is it plausible for us to reinvigorate this functionality and modernise it to align with contemporary workflow requirements?**
